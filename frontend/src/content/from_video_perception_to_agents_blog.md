# Seven Years of Teaching Machines to Watch

---

I've been working on video for seven years, from surveillance models in industry to agents that perceive and act in my PhD. Why video? Because the world is not text. Cooking, crossing a street, fixing a bike: almost everything that matters in daily life unfolds continuously in space and time and is never written down. If AI is going to help people in the physical world, it has to understand the world as it actually arrives: a continuous visual stream. This post is not a survey. It's how the ground shifted under me three times, and where I think it shifts next.

Before getting into the history, there's a contrast worth stating, because everything below depends on it. Text is naturally compressed: a discrete, human-built code where every token already carries meaning. Video is the opposite: continuous, low-level signal with no abstraction built in. The intelligence is in there, but nobody compressed it for us. The real question of video research has never been how to process more pixels; it's how to model the intelligence hidden behind them.

## When video was a classification problem

For most of the 2010s, video was images-with-a-time-axis. [Two-Stream Networks](https://arxiv.org/abs/1406.2199), [C3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/1412.0767), and [I3D](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.07750) defined the recipe: stack frames, learn spatiotemporal features, predict an action label. [SlowFast](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.03982) refined the architecture; [VideoMAE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.12602) fed it without labels. I lived this at SenseTime, where the one real landing point was surveillance: detect a fall, flag a fight, closed-set labels over fixed cameras.

The recipe worked well enough to ship, but there was something hollow about it. A model could ace action recognition with no idea why anything happened or what comes next. [**STAR**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.09711) (NeurIPS 2021) was my early push against this, representing situations symbolically so that "understanding" is something you can inspect. A bet on interpretability before the field could cash it. But the complaint held: recognition is not understanding.

## When language models cracked the door open

[CLIP](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020) put vision and language in one space; [Flamingo](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.14198) and [BLIP-2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.12597) made a frozen LLM reason over visual inputs. The question changed from "what action is this?" to "what can you conclude from this?" Then everyone hit the same wall: long video. Even a million-token context ([Gemini 1.5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.05530)) drowns in frame redundancy.

The way I came to see it, **long video understanding is mostly a retrieval problem.** Any question about an hour-long video is really about ninety seconds of it; the hard part is finding them. [**SeViLA**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06988) (NeurIPS 2023) built this in: the model localizes the relevant moments first, then reasons over them. That look–decide–answer loop is quietly agentic, the same realization [ViperGPT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.08128) reached from the tool-use side. The entire long-video literature since, including the test-time scaling wave after [o1](https://openai.com/index/learning-to-reason-with-llms/) and [DeepSeek-R1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948), is one theme: don't process the world; navigate it.

## When understanding started to demand action

World models went from curiosity ([Ha & Schmidhuber](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.10122)) to serious bet ([Genie](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.15391), [Sora-as-simulator](https://openai.com/index/video-generation-models-as-world-simulators/)); [RT-2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.15818) grounded VLMs into robot actions; [WebArena](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.13854) and [Mind2Web](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.06070) made digital action measurable.

What I care about in this transition is *grounding*: agents should act from genuine perception of a messy world, not clean abstractions. [**Ego2Web**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.22529) (CVPR 2026) connects egocentric video to web agents: watch through human eyes, then act online. [**AVIC**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.08236) pushes toward physical action, where the agent decides *when and how much to imagine* with a world model before moving. Imagination is expensive; budgeting your own thinking is itself an action. SeViLA's lesson generalizes: deciding what to do next (look, imagine, or act) is intelligence.

## Will world models be video's GPT moment?

I think AR + next-frame prediction is the right substrate: causal, streamable, action-conditionable, infinitely supervised by raw video. But two things make me cautious.

First, text arrives pre-compressed and video doesn't, so a next-frame model can spend its capacity on texture and still drive the loss down. The video GPT moment may be gated on a **tokenizer moment**: a latent space where "next token" means "next event," not "next patch."

Second, text found its product form early; video intelligence still hasn't. ChatGPT succeeded not just because of GPT-3, but because the chatbox was already a familiar interface used by billions of people every day. Video lacks an equivalent. Streaming generation and interactive worlds are promising directions, but the disappearance of Sora from public attention is a reminder that technical capability alone does not create a market. People may enjoy generated videos as a novelty, but novelty is different from utility. Unlike coding agents, which directly amplify productivity, or chatbots, which fit naturally into existing communication habits, video intelligence has yet to discover the interface that makes it indispensable. Three directions seem plausible to me:

- **Imagination as hidden compute.** The world model's output never reaches the user. It runs inside an agent as an internal simulator: before acting, the agent imagines a few possible futures, checks which action leads where, and only the chosen action is visible. This is the same pattern as coding agents, which run code behind the scenes and only show you the result. Robotics is the natural first market, since people already pay to train and test robot policies in simulation.
- **Fully open-world game generation.** Genie-style models that generate entire playable worlds on demand. This could be the consumer moment, because games are the one place where a world model's biggest weakness doesn't matter: a generated world only needs to feel right, not be physically correct. The risk on this path is regulatory rather than technical.
- **The streaming assistant.** Today's video QA assumes the video is finished: the model sees all of it, then answers. A real assistant works under the opposite conditions. The camera and microphone never stop, so it cannot store everything and must decide what to forget. But it also cannot reason from scratch on every frame; it needs a running internal model of what is happening and what will happen next. That is the world model's role: not generating video for the user to watch, but maintaining a silent, continuously updated prediction of the unfolding world—so the assistant knows when to respond, when to stay quiet, and when to step in unprompted. The right moment to respond is now, so a late answer is a wrong answer. Continuous video, audio, and eventually touch all feed this loop. Egocentric data ([Ego4D](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.07058), [EgoLife](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.03803)) is the only data that looks like this deployment condition.

The interface is already being attempted, but only halfway. Thinking Machines' [interaction models](https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/interaction-models/) train interactivity into the model natively, listening and speaking at the same time over continuous audio and video, and I think they genuinely solve the interface at the speech level. The harder gap is at the action level. Today, when a model wants to act, it still has to describe the action in natural language first: a textual plan, a tool call, a sentence like "pick up the cup." We simply haven't found an action representation that goes beyond language description, partly, I suspect, because robotics, the field that would force one into existence, is still maturing. But video intelligence is ultimately about fast reaction to the physical world. When a car rushes toward you, you don't narrate "the trajectory intersects mine." You just know, and you move. That reflex is video → action directly, with no words in between, and it is exactly what a world model should supply: a silent, continuous prediction of what happens next, driving immediate responses, with slow language-based reasoning called in only when the prediction is surprised. Routing every reaction through a sentence is a System-2 tax on a System-1 problem, and at the speed the real world moves, that tax is fatal.

History offers one useful lesson here. GPT-3 existed for two years before ChatGPT; the capability sat waiting until language found an interface that billions of people naturally understood. So I don't think anyone can predict which of the three directions above becomes video's defining product, and in a sense it doesn't matter. What matters is that all three depend on the same two underlying capabilities: action-conditioning reliable enough to build on, and fast, silent prediction you can trust. That is where the research effort should go. Prettier generated frames won't bring the moment closer, but whichever interface eventually wins, it will be built on those two foundations, and they aren't ready yet.
